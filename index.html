Stock Solution Preparation

Stock solutions for C12-200, DMPE-PEG2000, and DOPE can be prepared in advance and stored in the freezer for future use. PLGA stock must be prepared fresh on the day of formulation and discarded after use - it cannot be stored or reused.

The required amounts are calculated from the Excel sheet. Because the quantities are small and difficult to weigh precisely, weigh approximately the target amount, record the exact mass, and adjust the solvent volume accordingly.

Each stock is dissolved in its designated solvent:

  • C12-200 and DMPE-PEG2000: dissolve in 2% v/v acetic acid in acetonitrile.
  • DOPE: dissolve in absolute ethanol.
  • PLGA: dissolve in pure acetonitrile. Prepare fresh immediately before use.

Once all stocks are prepared, place the vials in a warm water bath at 50 °C for 2 minutes to ensure complete dissolution, then vortex briefly before use.

Preparation of the Organic Phase

The volumes of each stock are calculated from the Excel sheet. The addition order is critical: PLGA initiates nanoprecipitation and must be added last.

  1. Add the calculated excess volume of 2% v/v acetic acid in acetonitrile to a clean glass vial.
  2. Add the calculated volume of C12-200 stock solution.
  3. Add the calculated volume of DMPE-PEG2000 stock solution.
  4. Add the calculated volume of DOPE stock solution.
  5. Immediately before proceeding to microfluidic mixing, add the calculated volume of PLGA stock solution. Do not delay after this step.

2.3 Preparation of the Aqueous Phase (both formulations)

The aqueous phase is prepared identically for both formulations and both mRNA-loaded and empty LPN variants.

  • mRNA-loaded: Add the calculated volume of FLuc mRNA stock (1 mg/mL) to citrate buffer (pH 4.0) to reach the target mRNA concentration. Make up to the full aqueous phase volume with citrate buffer and mix gently by pipetting.
  • Empty particles: Replace the mRNA volume with an equal volume of citrate buffer (pH 4.0). The total aqueous phase volume remains the same.

3.2 Procedure
  1. Insert an Ignite NxGen cartridge into the Nanoassemblr instrument.
  2. Using a needle, transfer the organic phase into a 5 mL syringe and the aqueous phase into a 10 mL syringe.
  3. Remove all air bubbles from both syringes. Push the plunger up until the liquid reaches the very tip of the syringe with no air remaining.
  4. Insert each syringe into the instrument in the correct position as defined in the instrument settings (organic phase and aqueous phase channels).
  5. Insert the waste and sample collection vials (screw-cap vials) into their designated positions on the instrument.
  6. Before starting the run, set aside some PBS buffer in a separate tube for use in the upconcentration step later.
  7. Place the dialysis cassettes in the PBS buffer now to activate the membranes while the run proceeds (see Section 4).
  8. Start the Nanoassemblr run.
Important: Ensure no air bubbles remain in either syringe before inserting them into the instrument. Air in the syringe will disrupt the flow and compromise the formulation.
4 Dialysis ▶

Dialysis removes residual organic solvents (acetonitrile and ethanol) from the formulation and exchanges the citrate buffer (pH 4.0) for PBS (pH 7.4), cleaning up the formulation before downstream use.

Important: Never touch the dialysis membrane with your fingers and never allow it to dry at any point during this procedure. Handle the cassettes by their rigid frame only.
4.1 Procedure (Slide-A-Lyzer Dialysis Cassettes)
  1. While the Nanoassemblr run is in progress, place the dialysis cassettes in the beaker of PBS to hydrate and activate the membranes. Leave them submerged until the run is complete.
  2. Once the run is finished, draw 0.3 mL of air into a 5 mL syringe before aspirating the formulation from the sample vial. This air cushion prevents over-swelling of the membrane during loading.
  3. Inject the formulation through the top port of the cassette.
  4. Rinse the sample vial with a small volume of clean PBS and inject the washings into the cassette as well, to maximise recovery.
  5. Place the filled cassettes back into the beaker containing 1.5 L of PBS on a magnetic stirrer. The PBS should be stirring continuously throughout dialysis.
  6. Dialyse for 3 hours at room temperature on the magnetic stirrer.
  7. Transfer the beaker to a cold room (4 °C) and continue dialysing overnight, with stirring maintained.
  8. After dialysis, remove the cassettes from the beaker.
  9. Using a 5 mL syringe, extract the dialysed formulation from the bottom port of the cassette.
  10. Transfer the formulation to an appropriate tube for downstream use.
5 Upconcentration ▶

Upconcentration removes excess PBS that has entered the formulation during dialysis, reducing the volume to a manageable and concentrated final sample.

Important: Always use two filter vials in the centrifuge to balance the rotor. Never run with a single vial.
5.1 Activating the Filter Membranes
  1. Fill both centrifugal filter vials with PBS to activate the membranes: use clean PBS in the vial designated for the sample, and either clean or used dialysis PBS in the counterweight vial.
  2. Centrifuge both vials at 3000 × g, 4 °C for 5 minutes to wet the membranes.
  3. Remove the vials from the centrifuge and discard the liquid from the bottom collection tube of each vial.
Important: Never allow the membrane to dry during this procedure.
5.2 Concentrating the Sample
  1. Add a portion of the formulation to the top of the sample filter vial. Fill the counterweight vial with an equivalent volume of PBS to balance.
  2. Centrifuge at 3000 × g, 4 °C for 15 minutes.
  3. Repeat steps 4 and 5 - refill the filter vial with the next portion of formulation and centrifuge again - until all formulation has been loaded.
  4. Once all formulation has been added, continue centrifuging in 5-minute increments, checking the volume each time, until approximately 100 µL remains.
  5. Collect the concentrated formulation from the filter into 0.5 mL non-stick hydrophobic microtubes.
Important: Measure the exact recovered volume using a pipette: this value is required for downstream concentration calculations.
6 Dynamic light scattering (DLS) ▶

DLS is used to determine the hydrodynamic radius (Rh) and polydispersity index (PDI) of the LPN formulation. The hydrodynamic radius is defined as the radius of a hypothetical sphere that diffuses at the same rate as the particle, calculated from the measured diffusion coefficient D via the Stokes-Einstein relation:

rh = kT / (6πηD)

where k is Boltzmann's constant, T is the absolute temperature, and η is the viscosity of the solvent. Note that Rh does not give information about particle shape - it is a rough measure of effective size only.

Rh reports the average particle size in solution. The PDI describes the homogeneity of the size distribution: a PDI below 0.2 indicates a monodisperse, well-defined population. A PDI above 0.2 suggests a broad distribution with particles of significantly different sizes, which is generally undesirable.

6.1 Sample Preparation

The target concentration for DLS measurement is 0.18 mg/mL with a total cuvette volume of approximately 700 µL. The exact volume of sample V₁ to take is calculated from the Excel sheet based on the measured concentration after upconcentration, using:

C₁ · V₁ = C₂ · V₂

where C₁ is the LPN concentration after upconcentration (from the Excel sheet), C₂ = 0.18 mg/mL is the target measurement concentration, and V₂ ≈ 700 µL is the total cuvette volume. Make up the remaining volume with PBS.

6.3 Measurement Procedure
  1. Clean the cuvette by rinsing sequentially with Milli-Q water, ethanol, and Milli-Q water again. Allow to dry inverted before use.
  2. Calculate V₁ from the Excel sheet and prepare the sample in the cuvette: pipette V₁ of formulation and make up to ≈700 µL with PBS.
  3. Turn on the Zetasizer Nano and allow it to warm up fully.
  4. Insert the cuvette into the instrument.
  5. Open the Zetasizer software, create a new measurement file, and enter the settings listed above.
  6. Start the measurement run and wait for results.
  7. Export the data: save the Rh, PDI, and correlogram for each measurement.
7 RiboGreen assay ▶

The RiboGreen assay quantifies mRNA in solution and is used to determine two key quality metrics:

  • Encapsulation efficiency (EE%): the fraction of the initially added mRNA that is successfully encapsulated within the LPNs, determined by comparing free (unencapsulated) mRNA in the TE condition to total mRNA.
    EE% = ([RNA]total − [RNA]free) / [RNA]total × 100%
  • Recovery (%): the fraction of the originally added mRNA recovered after formulation, measured using Triton X-100 (TX) to disrupt the particles and release all encapsulated RNA.
    Recovery% = [RNA]total / [RNA]initially added × 100%

[RNA]total is measured in the presence of TX, which disrupts the LPNs and makes all RNA accessible to RiboGreen. [RNA]free is measured in TE only, reflecting unencapsulated RNA.

7.1 Buffer Volume Planning

Before preparing buffers, calculate the total volumes of 1×TE and 2% TX needed based on the number of samples. Each buffer is required for: the standard curve (6 wells × 3 repeats), one blank well, and the sample wells. Scale the buffer preparation accordingly.

7.2 Working Buffer Preparation
Working with Triton X-100 (TX): TX is highly viscous and prone to foaming. Always use reverse pipetting when handling TX: press the plunger all the way to the second stop before entering the liquid, aspirate by releasing the plunger fully, wait a moment, then withdraw the tip and dispense by pressing only to the first stop. This applies both when adding TX to TE buffer and when pipetting TX buffer into wells. Before starting the plate, vortex both the TE and TX buffers to ensure no residue has settled at the bottom.

1×TE buffer (50 mL): Prepare this twice - one batch will be used as 1×TE directly, and the second will be used to prepare the 2% TX buffer.

  1. Add 49.5 mL RNase-free water to a 50 mL tube.
  2. Add 0.5 mL of 100×TE buffer. Vortex.

2% Triton X-100 in TE (50 mL):

  1. Add 49 mL of 1×TE to a 50 mL tube.
  2. Add 1 mL of Triton X-100. Vortex.

2000× RiboGreen Working Dye (5 mL):

  1. Wrap the tube in aluminium foil to protect from light.
  2. Add 4997.5 µL of 1×TE to the tube.
  3. Add 2.5 µL of RiboGreen stock.
  4. Mix by gently shaking the tube by hand. Keep wrapped in foil and protected from light at all times.
7.3 Standard Curve Preparation
Note: Change the pipette tip between every step throughout the standard curve, blank, and sample preparation to avoid cross-contamination.

The standard curve is prepared twice - once using 1×TE buffer (for the TE condition) and once using 2% TX buffer (for the TX condition). Follow the exact same procedure below for each, substituting the appropriate buffer throughout. Each curve is run in triplicate (three full sets of wells 1–6).

The curve uses a 1:2 serial dilution in wells 1–6, prepared from a 100 ng/mL intermediate. Dilutions are split into two stages: in-tube and in-well.

In-tube dilutions

  1. Tube A: 1 µL mRNA stock (1 mg/mL) + 99 µL of the appropriate buffer (TE or TX) → 10 µg/mL
  2. Tube B: 5 µL Tube A + 495 µL of the appropriate buffer → 100 ng/mL

In-well serial dilution (from Tube B)

  1. Add 200 µL of Tube B (100 ng/mL) to well 1.
  2. Add 100 µL of the appropriate buffer to wells 2–6.
  3. Transfer 100 µL from well 1 to well 2; mix thoroughly by pipetting up and down. Change tip.
  4. Transfer 100 µL from well 2 to well 3; mix thoroughly. Change tip.
  5. Continue the 1:2 serial dilution through well 6, changing the tip at each transfer.
  6. After mixing well 6, remove and discard 100 µL so that each well contains exactly 100 µL.
7.4 Blank Wells
  • TE blank: 100 µL of 1×TE in a well.
  • TX blank: 100 µL of 2% TX in a well.
7.5 Sample Wells

Dilute samples to fall within the linear range of the standard curve. Typical working dilutions are approximately 1:4 000 for the TE condition and 1:20 000 for the TX condition. Refer to the Excel sheet for the exact volumes based on your sample concentration.

7.6 Adding RiboGreen Dye and Reading the Plate

Once all wells (standards, blanks, and samples) are prepared, add 100 µL of RiboGreen working dye to every well. Mix each well thoroughly by pipetting up and down, changing the tip between wells. Read the plate immediately, keeping it protected from light until measurement.

8 Asymmetric flow field-flow fractionation (AF4) ▶

AF4 is used to characterise the size distribution and separation of LPN populations.

8.1 Sample Preparation

Dilute the sample to a nominal concentration of 4 mg/mL in PBS, using the nominal concentration calculated in the Excel sheet after upconcentration. It is also useful to cross-check the concentration using the RiboGreen results.

Note: A nominal concentration down to 2 mg/mL has been shown to work well, but 4 mg/mL is the standard.

Transfer the diluted sample into small glass vials with a pierceable (permeable) top, so that the instrument needle can go through for injection.

Important: Sample can collect on the walls and top of the vial rather than pooling at the bottom, which means less sample than expected may be recovered. Before loading, briefly centrifuge the vials for a few seconds to bring all the sample to the bottom. This step is critical to ensure the full sample volume is available for injection and nothing is wasted.
8.2 Injection Parameters

The standard injection volume is 220 µL total: 200 µL sample injection with 20 µL excess. If sample volume is limited, injections down to 70 µL have been shown to work well, though 220 µL is the standard.

8.3 Online AF4-SAXS Measurements

For online AF4-SAXS measurements, the same sample preparation and injection settings apply: dilute to a nominal concentration of 4 mg/mL in PBS and use a 220 µL injection volume (200 µL + 20 µL excess).

In addition, extra PBS buffer is required as a carrier and for flushing between samples. 1 L has been found to be more than sufficient for 3 samples.

AF4 chromatogram plotter

Reads AF4 instrument Excel exports · plots UV 260 nm and/or Rh traces · auto-detects samples from header
Download script

What the script does

Parses the header row of an AF4 Excel export to detect sample names and available channels (UV / Rh), then plots the selected chromatograms with peak annotations and dotted vertical lines at each UV peak maximum.

Required input format - direct AF4 instrument export (.xlsx)

The script reads the Excel file as exported directly by the AF4 instrument software. No manual reformatting needed. The header row must contain column names in the format:

time (min) | 1126 - F3 empty 200uL Vcf175 Vx250g Vf000 (UV) | time (min).1 | 1126 - F3 empty 200uL ... (Rh(Q)) | ...

Duplicate time (min) columns are renamed automatically by pandas (.1, .2, …). The script pairs each UV/Rh column with its preceding time column.

How to run

  1. Open AF4_UV_Rh_plotting_FINAL.py in your editor.
  2. Set the xlsx_path variable inside main() to your file path.
  3. Run: python AF4_UV_Rh_plotting_FINAL.py
  4. The terminal will list detected samples - enter numbers to select, or press Enter for all.
  5. Choose channel: UV, Rh, or both.

Outputs

  • Interactive matplotlib figure (UV and/or Rh overlays)
  • PNG file saved next to the input Excel file (_af4_plot.png)
  • Peak time annotated in legend labels for UV channel

ChromIXS frame processing

Buffer/sample region detection · spike rejection · buffer subtraction · averaged block export
Download script

What the script does

Reads a folder of individual SAXS frames (.dat files), builds an intensity-vs-frame chromatogram trace, and lets you select buffer and sample regions (automatically, manually, or interactively with a slider). It then averages buffer frames (with spike rejection), subtracts the buffer from each sample block, and saves the subtracted curves as new .dat files.

Required input - folder of .dat files

Each file is one SAXS frame in three-column format:

# optional comment lines q I(q) error 1.234e-02 5.678e+00 1.234e-01 ...

Files are read in natural (numerical) order by filename. The q grid must be identical across all frames.

How to run

  1. Open Chromixs.py and set FOLDER to your data directory.
  2. Choose SELECTION_MODE: "auto" (recommended first try), "manual", or "slider".
  3. Set WINDOW_SIZE (frames per averaged output curve, default 50) and STEP (default 50 = non-overlapping blocks).
  4. Run: python Chromixs.py
  5. If using "auto" or "manual", a confirmation plot is shown - press Enter to accept or r to switch to the interactive slider.

Key settings

FOLDER = Path("your/data/folder") WINDOW_SIZE = 50 # frames per output curve STEP = 50 # 50 = non-overlapping; 1 = sliding window SELECTION_MODE = "auto" # "auto" | "manual" | "slider" EXPORT_REMAINDER = True # include last incomplete block? # Auto-detection tuning AUTO_BASELINE_FRACTION = 0.15 # fraction of initial frames for baseline AUTO_PEAK_FRACTION = 0.005 # threshold = baseline + this × (peak − baseline) AUTO_SMOOTH_WINDOW = 30 # smoothing window before thresholding BUFFER_SPIKE_SIGMA = 5.0 # exclude buffer frames > median + N×std

Outputs

  • Subtracted .dat files saved to a new subfolder (_subtracted_50frame_blocks)
  • Each file named with sample and buffer frame ranges
  • Summary chromatogram plot with error bars saved as PNG

Guinier analysis

Automatic Guinier range fitting · Rg ± error · I₀ · qRg validation · batch over folder
Download script

What the script does

Reads all .dat files in a folder, searches the low-q region of each for the best Guinier window (maximising R², number of points, and qRg close to 1.0 while keeping qRg,max < 1.3), fits a weighted linear regression to ln(I) vs q², and extracts Rg ± error and I₀. Results are saved to a CSV table.

Required input - folder of .dat files

Same three-column format as ChromIXS (q, I, error). Files are processed in natural numerical order. Only positive-intensity data points are used.

q I(q) error 1.234e-02 5.678e+00 1.234e-01 ...

How to run

  1. Open Guinier.py and set FOLDER to your folder of subtracted frames.
  2. Adjust Guinier criteria if needed (see settings below).
  3. Run: python Guinier.py
  4. Results are printed to the terminal and saved to guinier_results.csv in the same folder.

Key settings

FOLDER = Path("your/subtracted/folder") MIN_POINTS = 6 # minimum points in Guinier window MAX_POINTS = 30 # maximum points in Guinier window QRG_MAX_LIMIT = 1.30 # strict Guinier criterion (qRg,max must be < this) LOWQ_FRACTION = 0.25 # only search lowest 25% of q-range MIN_R2 = 0.995 # minimum R² for a valid window

Outputs

  • guinier_results.csv - Rg, Rg_err, qmin, qmax, qRg_max, I₀, R² per file
  • Terminal summary of all fits (OK / failed with reason)

How to run

  1. Open Offline%20batch%20samples.py and set FOLDER to your data directory.
  2. Edit FILES_TO_PLOT - list the file stems (without extension) you want to include.
  3. Set EXTENSION = ".dat" (or ".txt" as appropriate).
  4. Set USE_LOGLOG = True for log-log, False for linear.
  5. Run: python Offline%20batch%20samples.py

Outputs

  • Interactive matplotlib figure with all selected I(q) curves
About

I work on the biophysical characterization of lipid polymer nanoparticles (LPNs) for mRNA delivery, with a focus on formulation, size, structure, and encapsulation efficiency.

This site collects the protocols and analysis scripts developed during my work at the Department of Pharmacy, University of Copenhagen. Methods include microfluidic LPN assembly, AF4 fractionation, SAXS, RiboGreen assays, and custom Python tooling for data processing and visualization.

If you have questions about any of the methods, would like access to the scripts, or are interested in collaborating, please get in touch.
