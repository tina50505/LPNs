Lipid Polymer Nanoparticles - Preparation & Characterisation

LPN · mRNA Microfluidics DLS · AF4 · SAXS Tina Jurkošek

Clean the bench with RNaseZAP before starting. Wear gloves at all times when handling RNA-containing solutions. Use RNase-free consumables throughout.

1 Buffer preparation ▶
1.1 Phosphate-Buffered Saline (PBS, 1.5 L, 20 mM, pH 7.4)

Reagents

Procedure

  1. Weigh all salts as listed above.
  2. Transfer the salts into a clean 2 L beaker (see beaker cleaning procedure below).
  3. Add 1.5 L of Milli-Q water.
  4. Mix on a magnetic stirrer until all salts are fully dissolved.
Important: Verify the pH of the buffer the following day using a calibrated pH meter.

Beaker Cleaning Procedure

  1. Wipe the beaker thoroughly with RNaseZAP and rinse with demineralised water.
  2. Bake the beaker in an oven at 180 °C for a minimum of 6 hours.
1.2 Citrate Buffer (50 mL, 100 mM, pH 4.0)

Reagents

ReagentAmount
Sodium citrate dihydrate248.22 mg

RNase-free waterup to 50 mL

Procedure

  1. Weigh out the sodium citrate dihydrate and citric acid and transfer both into a 50 mL falcon tube.
  2. Add RNase-free water to approximately 40 mL and vortex until completely dissolved.
  3. Make up to exactly 50 mL with RNase-free water.
  4. Sterile-filter the buffer through a 0.45 µm membrane filter before use.
Important: Verify the pH of the buffer the following day using a calibrated pH meter.

Stock solutions for C12-200, DMPE-PEG2000, and DOPE can be prepared in advance and stored in the freezer for future use. PLGA stock must be prepared fresh on the day of formulation and discarded after use - it cannot be stored or reused.

Each stock is dissolved in its designated solvent:

  • C12-200 and DMPE-PEG2000: dissolve in 2% v/v acetic acid in acetonitrile.
  • DOPE: dissolve in absolute ethanol.
  • PLGA: dissolve in pure acetonitrile. Prepare fresh immediately before use.

The addition order is critical: PLGA initiates nanoprecipitation and must be added last.

  1. Add the calculated excess volume of 2% v/v acetic acid in acetonitrile to a clean glass vial.
  2. Add the calculated volume of C12-200 stock solution.
  3. Add the calculated volume of DMPE-PEG2000 stock solution.
  4. Add the calculated volume of DOPE stock solution.
  5. Immediately before proceeding to microfluidic mixing, add the calculated volume of PLGA stock solution. Do not delay after this step.

2.3 Preparation of the Aqueous Phase (both formulations)
  • mRNA-loaded: Add the calculated volume of FLuc mRNA stock (1 mg/mL) to citrate buffer (pH 4.0). Make up to the full aqueous phase volume with citrate buffer and mix gently.
  • Empty particles: Replace the mRNA volume with an equal volume of citrate buffer (pH 4.0).
3 Microfluidic assembly (Nanoassemblr Ignite) ▶
Flow rate ratio (aqueous : organic)3:1
Total flow rate12 mL/min
Start waste volume0.25 mL
End waste volume0.10 mL
Total processed volume12 mL
  1. Insert an Ignite NxGen cartridge into the Nanoassemblr instrument.
  2. Transfer the organic phase into a 5 mL syringe and the aqueous phase into a 10 mL syringe using a needle.
  3. Remove all air bubbles from both syringes.
  4. Insert each syringe into the instrument in the correct position.
  5. Insert the waste and sample collection vials into their designated positions.
  6. Place the dialysis cassettes in PBS buffer now to activate the membranes (see Section 4).
  7. Start the Nanoassemblr run.
Important: Ensure no air bubbles remain in either syringe before inserting them. Air will disrupt the flow and compromise the formulation.
4 Dialysis ▶

Dialysis removes residual organic solvents and exchanges citrate buffer (pH 4.0) for PBS (pH 7.4).

Important: Never touch the dialysis membrane with your fingers and never allow it to dry. Handle cassettes by their rigid frame only.
  1. While the run is in progress, place dialysis cassettes in PBS to hydrate the membranes.
  2. Draw 0.3 mL of air into a 5 mL syringe before aspirating the formulation. This air cushion prevents over-swelling during loading.
  3. Inject the formulation through the top port of the cassette.
  4. Rinse the sample vial with a small volume of PBS and inject washings into the cassette.
  5. Place filled cassettes in 1.5 L PBS on a magnetic stirrer.
  6. Dialyse for 3 hours at room temperature.
  7. Transfer to cold room (4 °C) and continue dialysing overnight.
  8. Extract the dialysed formulation from the bottom port using a 5 mL syringe.
5 Upconcentration ▶

Removes excess PBS from dialysis, reducing volume to a concentrated final sample.

Important: Always use two filter vials in the centrifuge to balance the rotor.
  1. Fill both centrifugal filter vials with PBS to activate membranes.
  2. Centrifuge at 3000 × g, 4 °C for 5 minutes. Discard filtrate.
  3. Add formulation to the sample filter vial; fill counterweight vial with PBS to balance.
  4. Centrifuge at 3000 × g, 4 °C for 15 minutes.
  5. Repeat until all formulation is loaded, then centrifuge in 5-minute increments until ≈00 µL remains.
  6. Collect the concentrated formulation into 0.5 mL non-stick hydrophobic microtubes.
Important: Measure the exact recovered volume — this is required for downstream concentration calculations.
6 Dynamic light scattering (DLS) ▶

DLS measures hydrodynamic radius (Rh) and PDI via the Stokes-Einstein relation: rh = kT / (6πηD). PDI below 0.2 indicates a monodisperse population.

Target concentration: 0.18 mg/mL in ≈00 µL total. Calculate V₁ using C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ from the Excel sheet.

  1. Rinse cuvette with Milli-Q water, ethanol, and Milli-Q water again. Allow to dry.
  2. Prepare sample at 0.18 mg/mL in the cuvette.
  3. Start measurement and export Rh, PDI, and correlogram for each run.
7 RiboGreen assay ▶

Quantifies mRNA encapsulation efficiency (EE%) and recovery:

  • EE% = ([RNA]total − [RNA]free) / [RNA]total × 100%
  • Recovery% = [RNA]total / [RNA]initially added × 100%

[RNA]total is measured with Triton X-100 (TX) to disrupt particles. [RNA]free is measured in TE only.

Working with TX: Always use reverse pipetting. Vortex both TE and TX buffers before starting the plate.

Buffer preparation: 1×TE: 0.5 mL 100×TE + 49.5 mL RNase-free water. 2% TX: 1 mL TX + 49 mL 1×TE. 2000× RiboGreen dye: 2.5 µL stock + 4997.5 µL 1×TE (protect from light).

Standard curve: Prepare in both TE and TX (triplicate). Serial dilution: Tube A: 1 µL mRNA (1 mg/mL) + 99 µL buffer → 10 µg/mL. Tube B: 5 µL Tube A + 495 µL buffer → 100 ng/mL. Then 1:2 serial dilution in-well across wells 1–6.

ConditionTube ATube BTotal dilution
TE1 µL sample + 99 µL TE10 µL Tube A + 390 µL TE~1:4 000
TX1 µL sample + 99 µL TX5 µL Tube A + 995 µL TX~1:20 000

Add 100 µL RiboGreen dye to all wells. Read immediately, protected from light.

8 Asymmetric flow field-flow fractionation (AF4) ▶

AF4 characterises the size distribution and separation of LPN populations.

Dilute the sample to 4 mg/mL in PBS. Transfer into glass vials with a pierceable top. Briefly centrifuge vials before loading to bring sample to the bottom. Standard injection: 220 µL (200 µL sample + 20 µL excess).

Note: For online AF4-SAXS, same settings apply. 1 L PBS is sufficient as carrier for 3 samples.
9 CoSAXS BioCube measurements ▶

Dilute the sample to 1 mg/mL in PBS. Detector distance: 3.5 m.

RiboGreen analysis

Standard curve fitting · EE% · recovery · replicate QC · multi-file batch processing
Open script

What the script does

Reads one or more Excel files (each containing a sheet named Data_for_Python), fits a linear standard curve per plate and replicate, calculates mRNA concentrations for all sample wells, and computes encapsulation efficiency and recovery. Outputs plots and an Excel/CSV results table.

Required Excel sheet format - sheet name: Data_for_Python

ColumnTypeDescription
platetextPlate name / condition, e.g. TE or TX
welltextWell identifier, e.g. A1. Row letter groups replicates.
sampletextSample name, e.g. F3
buffertextBuffer name, e.g. TE or PBS
fractionnumberFraction number from AF4
kindtextOne of: standard, blank, sample, exclude
std_conc_ng_mlnumberStandard concentration in ng/mL. Leave blank for sample and blank rows.
dilution_xnumberDilution factor as a plain number, e.g. 20 or 50
rfunumberMeasured fluorescence (RFU)
notetextOptional free-text note, e.g. 20x dilution
Download Excel template

How to run

  1. Open the script in your editor.
  2. Edit the FILES list to point to your Excel files.
  3. Set OUTDIR to the folder where you want plots and tables saved.
  4. Run the script.

Outputs

  • Standard curve plots per plate
  • Encapsulation efficiency (EE%) curve per sample type
  • ribogreen_clean_output_table.xlsx - full results with flags
  • curve_summary.csv - slope, intercept, R² per plate

Key settings in the script

FILES = [Path("your_file.xlsx"), ...] SHEET = "Data_for_Python" OUTDIR = Path("output_folder") MIN_REPLICATE_R2_FOR_AVG = 0.99 SATURATION_RFU = 260000 CLIP_NEGATIVE_CONC_TO_ZERO = True

AF4 chromatogram plotter

Reads AF4 instrument Excel exports · plots UV 260 nm and/or Rh traces · auto-detects samples from header
Open script

What the script does

Parses the header row of an AF4 Excel export to detect sample names and available channels (UV / Rh), then plots the selected chromatograms with peak annotations and dotted vertical lines at each UV peak maximum.

Required input format - direct AF4 instrument export (.xlsx)

The script reads the Excel file as exported directly by the AF4 instrument. No manual reformatting needed. The header row must contain column names in the format:

time (min) | 1126 - F3 empty 200uL Vcf175 Vx250g Vf000 (UV) | time (min).1 | ... (Rh(Q)) | ...

How to run

  1. Open the script and set the xlsx_path variable inside main().
  2. Run the script.
  3. The terminal lists detected samples , enter numbers to select, or press Enter for all.
  4. Choose channel: UV, Rh, or both.

Outputs

  • Interactive matplotlib figure (UV and/or Rh overlays)
  • PNG saved next to the input file (_af4_plot.png)
  • Peak time annotated in legend labels for UV channel

DLS plotter

Size distribution and correlogram plotting from Zetasizer .txt exports · auto-detects samples and repeats · interactive selection
Open script

What the script does

Points to a folder of Zetasizer .txt exports and automatically detects which files are correlograms and which are size distributions. Sample names and repeat numbers are extracted from the record header row (e.g. Record 4: F3 empty 1 → sample F3 empty, repeat 1). At runtime the script asks which plot type(s) to produce and which samples to include; all repeats for a chosen sample are overlaid on the same figure.

Required input - folder of Zetasizer .txt exports

The folder must contain tab-separated .txt files whose names include Correlogram or Size Dist (case-insensitive). Expected column format:

X Lag Time Record 1: F3 mRNA 1 Record 2: F3 mRNA 2 ... 0.5 0.912 0.910 ... 1.0 0.917 0.915 ...

How to run

  1. Open DLS FINAL.py and set DATA_FOLDER to your .txt files folder.
  2. Optionally set SAVE_FOLDER (or leave None to display only).
  3. Run the script.
  4. Choose plot type (correlogram / size dist / both).
  5. Choose samples , enter numbers or press Enter for all.

Key settings in the script

DATA_FOLDER = r"C:\path o\DLS\data" # folder with .txt files SAVE_FOLDER = None # path to save figures, or None SAVE_FORMAT = "png" # "png", "svg", or "pdf" SAVE_DPI = 300

Outputs

  • One figure per selected sample with all repeats overlaid
  • Figures saved to SAVE_FOLDER if set (e.g. SampleName_correlogram.png)

Chromixs frame processing

Buffer/sample region detection · spike rejection · buffer subtraction · averaged block export
Open script

What the script does

Reads a folder of individual SAXS frames (.dat files), builds an intensity-vs-frame chromatogram trace, and lets you select buffer and sample regions (automatically, manually, or with a slider). Averages buffer frames (with spike rejection), subtracts the buffer from each sample block, and saves the subtracted curves as new .dat files.

Required input - folder of .dat files

q I(q) error 1.234e-02 5.678e+00 1.234e-01 ...

How to run

  1. Open Chromixs.py and set FOLDER to your data directory.
  2. Choose SELECTION_MODE: "auto", "manual", or "slider".
  3. Run the script.

Key settings

FOLDER = Path("your/data/folder") WINDOW_SIZE = 50 # frames per output curve STEP = 50 # 50 = non-overlapping blocks SELECTION_MODE = "auto" # "auto" | "manual" | "slider" BUFFER_SPIKE_SIGMA = 5.0 # exclude buffer frames > median + N×std

Outputs

  • Subtracted .dat files in a new subfolder (_subtracted_50frame_blocks)
  • Summary chromatogram plot saved as PNG

Guinier analysis

Automatic Guinier fitting · longest-stable-window criterion · Rg ± error · I₀ · qRg validation · p(r) readiness flags · batch over folder
Open script

What the script does

Reads all .dat files in a folder and searches the low-q region of each for a valid Guinier window. Windows must pass hard rejection criteria (slope < 0, qRg,max ≤ 1.3, R² ≥ MIN_R2) and a stability criterion: from all windows whose Rg CV ≤ MAX_RG_CV, the longest is selected. If no window meets the CV threshold the script falls back to the window with the lowest CV. After fitting, each file receives a next_step flag (p(r), inspect_then_pr, or recheck) indicating readiness for downstream p(r) analysis.

Required input - folder of .dat files

q I(q) error 1.234e-02 5.678e+00 1.234e-01 ...

How to run

  1. Open the script and set INPUT_FOLDER to your subtracted frames folder.
  2. Adjust fitting criteria if needed.
  3. Run the script.
  4. Results are printed to terminal and saved to a dated subfolder.

Key settings

INPUT_FOLDER = r"your/subtracted/folder" MAX_LOW_Q_POINTS = 60 # max low-q points to search MAX_LOW_Q_CUTOFF = 0.10 # only search q < this value (Å⁻¹) MIN_WINDOW_POINTS = 5 # minimum points in a fit window MAX_WINDOW_POINTS = 30 # maximum points in a fit window QRG_LIMIT = 1.3 # hard upper bound on qmax·Rg MIN_R2 = 0.990 # minimum R² for a valid window MAX_RG_CV = 0.05 # stability: CV of Rg across overlapping windows

Outputs

  • guinier_results.csv , Rg, Rg_err, qmin, qmax, qRg_max, I₀, R², stability_cv, next_step per file
  • 4-panel diagnostic PNG per file (_guinier.png): Guinier plot, I(q), Rg across windows, residuals
  • Terminal summary with pass/fail reason per file

p(r) analysis via datgnom

Batch datgnom runs · Rg from Guinier CSV · .out file output · summary CSV · autorg fallback
Open script

What the script does

Reads the guinier_results.csv produced by the Guinier script and selects files flagged with next_step = p(r) or inspect_then_pr. For each selected file it calls ATSAS datgnom with the corresponding Rg, saves the resulting .out file, and writes a summary CSV with Dmax, Rg_pr, I₀, and quality flags. An optional autorg fallback handles files where the Guinier fit failed.

Prerequisites

ATSAS must be installed (tested with ATSAS 3.1.3). Set DATGNOM_EXE and AUTORG_EXE to the correct paths. Run the Guinier script first on the same folder to produce guinier_results.csv.

How to run

  1. Run the Guinier script first to generate guinier_results.csv.
  2. Open p(r).py and set BASE_FOLDER and SAMPLE_FOLDER.
  3. Verify DATGNOM_EXE and AUTORG_EXE paths are correct for your machine.
  4. Run the script.

Key settings

BASE_FOLDER = Path(r"C:\path o\your\data") SAMPLE_FOLDER = "your_subtracted_folder" DATGNOM_EXE = r"C:\Program Files\ATSAS-3.1.3in\datgnom.exe" AUTORG_EXE = r"C:\Program Files\ATSAS-3.1.3inutorg.exe" AUTORG_FALLBACK = True # use autorg if Guinier Rg is missing AUTORG_FALLBACK_ALL = False # also attempt p(r) on Guinier-failed files INCLUDE_INSPECT_THEN_PR = True # include "inspect_then_pr" files DATGNOM_TIMEOUT = 300 # seconds per file

Outputs

  • One subfolder per processed file containing the .out file and datgnom logs
  • datgnom_summary.csv , Dmax, Rg_pr, Rg_pr_err, I₀, rg_source, datgnom_status, pr_recommendation per file
  • matched_files_with_rg.csv , file-matching diagnostic table

Power-law analysis

I(q) ∝ q−α · automatic region search · stability criterion · two-regime detection · diagnostic plots · batch over folder
Open script

What the script does

Fits a power law I(q) ∝ q−α in the intermediate- and high-q regime for every SAXS file in a folder. The exponent α carries physical meaning: α = 4 is the Porod law (smooth interfaces), α = 2 a Gaussian coil, α = 1 a thin rod, α ∈ (1,3) a mass fractal, α ∈ (3,4) a surface fractal.

The fitting region is found automatically by evaluating all candidate windows within the user-defined q range by weighted linear regression of ln(I) vs ln(q). Windows must satisfy R² ≥ MIN_R2 and a stability criterion (CV of α across overlapping windows ≤ MAX_ALPHA_CV); the longest stable window is selected. A two-regime check is performed using an F-test. If a guinier_results.csv is provided, the per-file Guinier qmax is used as the lower q boundary.

Required input - folder of .dat files

q I(q) error 1.234e-02 5.678e+00 1.234e-01 ...

How to run

  1. Open the script and set BASE_FOLDER and SAMPLE_FOLDER.
  2. Optionally point GUINIER_CSV to your guinier_results.csv for automatic per-file q-boundaries.
  3. Run the script.
  4. Results printed to terminal and saved to a dated subfolder.

Key settings

BASE_FOLDER = Path(r"C:\path o\your\data") SAMPLE_FOLDER = "your_subtracted_folder" Q_MIN_FIT = 0.02 # lower q boundary (Å⁻¹); overridden per-file if GUINIER_CSV set Q_MAX_FIT = None # upper q boundary; None = noise cutoff only MIN_WINDOW_POINTS = 10 MAX_WINDOW_POINTS = 100 MIN_R2 = 0.990 MAX_ALPHA_CV = 0.05 # stability: CV of α across overlapping windows MAX_RELATIVE_ERROR = 0.5 # exclude points where σ/I exceeds this GUINIER_CSV = INPUT_FOLDER / "guinier_results.csv" # None to disable F_TEST_ALPHA = 0.05 # significance threshold for two-regime detection

Outputs

  • powerlaw_results.csv , α, α_err, α₂, q_transition, qmin, qmax, R², χ²_red, stability_cv, two_regime, status per file
  • 4-panel diagnostic PNG per file (_powerlaw.png): log-log I(q) with fit overlay, residuals, α across valid windows
About

I work on the biophysical characterization of lipid polymer nanoparticles (LPNs) for mRNA delivery, with a focus on formulation, size, structure, and encapsulation efficiency.

This site collects the protocols and analysis scripts developed during my work at the Department of Pharmacy, University of Copenhagen. Methods include microfluidic LPN assembly, AF4 fractionation, SAXS, RiboGreen assays, and custom Python tooling for data processing and visualization.
